Research Overview

Climate risks are wicked problems. They arise from interacting environmental, social, cultural, institutional, historical, and economic forces, and they do not yield to single-discipline solutions. My research bridges theory and practice in climate risk and socioecological systems by integrating process-based hydrologic modeling, geospatial statistics, remote sensing, and agent-based simulation, grounded in community-based participatory research. This approach lets me trace feedbacks among hydroclimatic extremes, the built environment, and human behavior; analyze governance and risk perceptions that shape coordination; and produce decision tools for just and sustainable adaptation from neighborhood to national scales. Trained in hydrological science, water resources management, and mathematical modeling, and collaborating across geography, planning, social science, and sustainability, I explain past change, test plausible futures, and identify leverage points that reduce risk while advancing equity.

1) Community-Engaged Urban Climate Resilience

Objective. Co-produce actionable knowledge and multi-scale evidence with residents and partners that aligns lived experience with hydrologic and spatial analysis, builds shared capacity, and converts insight into plans and policies that strengthen climate resilience.

Community-based approaches to urban pluvial flooding (UPF)

In my PhD research, I examined how residents can be more meaningfully engaged in addressing UPF. Azizi et al. (2022) highlight that many multidisciplinary approaches treat residents mainly as data collectors, which limits reciprocal learning and overlooks resident knowledge that can improve flood models. We argue that effective UPF research must integrate technical analysis with social, political, and economic realities at the neighborhood scale.

Figure 1. Trends in community-based UPF research.

As a case study, Azizi et al. (2023) engaged residents in Germantown, Tennessee, after a major 2019 flood. By combining hydrologic–hydrodynamic modeling with multi-level community-based data collection, the project improved the basin model and fostered two-way learning, strengthening residents’ understanding of their own risks.

Figure 2. Participatory UPF methodology and modeled flood extent (June 7, 2019).

Timing matters. Residents are often more responsive shortly after a flood, while proactive engagement is needed where recent events are absent.

Figure 3. Engagement framework for outreach before vs. after extreme events.

Project CRESSLE (Community Resilience integrated into an Earth System Science Learning Ecosystem)

This project partners UT researchers with Austin neighborhoods to assess community needs and build a community of practice that designs and implements community-driven research on resilience and sustainability. Research is organized around four themes: Water Resources, Climate Resilience, Communities and Land Use, and Air Quality.

  • Assessing community exposure to climate impacts (heat & rainfall).
  • Analyzing social vulnerability to understand disproportionate impacts.
  • Integrating downscaled geophysical data with social indicators of resilience.

2) Sustainability Transitions in Urban Environmental Systems

Objective. Study just and sustainable transition pathways by linking system-dynamics and geospatial analytics to address vulnerability, and use this information to design risk management strategies that are scientifically sound, technologically feasible, economically efficient, and ethically defensible.

Water Resources Management and Water Conservation

This research examines how urban water systems perform under short- and long-term stressors and how policy choices shape equity and affordability. I built system-dynamics models for 16 large U.S. water utilities capturing feedbacks among supply, demand, infrastructure, finance, and service reliability. Qualitative comparative analysis identifies configurations of environmental, institutional, social, and financial attributes that support both affordability and quality.

Figure 5. NNMF of conditions and cases (two components).

Building on this systems view, I evaluated conservation programs with spatial and statistical analysis. In Miami-Dade County, a high-efficiency toilet rebate reduced demand in high-consumption areas but disproportionately benefited higher-income households. In Las Vegas Valley Water District, portfolio evaluation under dual objectives (savings & affordability) revealed trade-offs and synergies when both objectives are integrated.

Figure 6. Pareto frontier between water savings and affordability under a fixed budget.

Multi-Sectoral Insights into U.S. Gulf Coast Flood Governance & Social Network Analysis

Jefferson County (TX) & Baldwin County (AL): interviews + Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping (FCM) reveal a technical-infrastructure frame vs. an equity-oriented frame. Misalignment produces collaboration gaps and fragmented risk reduction.

Effective coastal flood governance aligns infrastructure investments with equity-focused interventions, fosters shared framing, and empowers less-connected actors to bridge gaps.

3) Coupled Human–Environment Modeling and Spatial Analysis

Objective. Integrate mixed-methods data and built-environment information into dynamical and spatial-network models that analyze and predict human–environment interaction, map sustainable pathways, and optimize policy targeting so that resource management and hazard mitigation scale equitably.

Socio-Behavioral & Spatial Dynamics in Green Stormwater Infrastructure (GSI) Adoption

To address uneven adoption, I integrate surveys, structural equation modeling, and agent-based modeling in the Austin region. Household norms, awareness, attitudes, and social capital are coupled with parcel-level hydrologic and spatial attributes to parameterize an ABM of GSI diffusion.

Figure 10. ABM framework for GSI adoption.

The model reproduces observed patterns, identifies where incentives vs. outreach are most effective, and aligns geophysical data with social vulnerability indicators to guide equitable targeting.

Figure 11. Baselines and simulated adoption across districts.

Next, I examine how adaptation preferences evolve along exposure gradients, develop distance-weighted indices and survey-informed ABMs to detect tipping points, and test procedural justice by embedding participatory budgeting alongside conventional rebates to support high-risk, low-income households.
