Line 1: Community-Engaged Urban Climate Resilience

The primary objective is to co-produce actionable knowledge and multi-scale evidence with residents and partners that aligns lived experience with hydrologic and spatial analysis, builds shared capacity, and converts insight into plans and policies that strengthen small- and large-scale climate resilience.

Project: Community-based approaches to Urban Pluvial Flooding (UPF) research

In my PhD research, I examined how residents can be more meaningfully engaged in addressing UPF. Azizi et al. (2022) highlight that many multi-disciplinary approaches treat residents mainly as data collectors, missing opportunities for reciprocal learning and overlooking their knowledge in improving flood models. We argued that effective UPF research must integrate technical analysis with social, political, and economic realities at the community level.

Figure 1 summarizes trends in community-based UPF research (Azizi et al., 2022).

As a case study, Azizi et al. (2023) engaged residents in Germantown, Tennessee, after a major 2019 flood. By combining hydrologic–hydrodynamic modeling with multi-level community-based data collection, the project both improved the flood model and fostered two-way learning, strengthening residents’ understanding of their own risks.

Figure 2 illustrates the participatory UPF methodology and the modeled flooding extent for the June 7, 2019 event.

Our work also showed that the timing of engagement matters. Residents are often more responsive shortly after a flood, while proactive engagement is needed in places without recent events. Figure 3 presents a framework for tailoring engagement based on whether outreach occurs before or after an extreme event.

While challenges remain—such as sustaining participation and reaching a critical mass of residents—the findings demonstrate that citizen science can reduce data gaps and enhance local resilience in diverse contexts.

Project CRESSLE — Community Resilience integrated into an Earth System Science Learning Ecosystem

This project partners UT researchers with Austin neighborhoods to assess community needs and establish a community of practice that designs and implements community-driven research projects on resilience and sustainability challenges. Research is organized around four themes: Water Resources, Climate Resilience, Communities and Land Use, and Air Quality.

My focus: climate resilience, addressing two major urban challenges—extreme heat and extreme precipitation/flooding. Key elements include:

  1. Assessing community exposure to climate impacts (heat and rainfall) as functions of atmospheric forcing, geography, and land cover.
  2. Analyzing social vulnerability to understand how identical drivers produce disproportionate impacts across urban areas.
  3. Investigating strategic solutions by integrating downscaled geophysical data with social indicators of community resilience.
